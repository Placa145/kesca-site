NFPA 96 — required cleaning intervals

Most operators do not know their required interval. Most cleaners do not confirm it. That is part of the problem.

Solid-fuel cooking — wood, charcoalMonthly
High-volume — 24-hour, charbroiling, wokMonthly
Moderate-volume — standard sit-down restaurantQuarterly
Low-volume — seasonal, churches, camps, day programsSemi-annual
Light prep — minimal cookingAnnual

Intervals from NFPA 96 Table 11.4. Your local authority having jurisdiction has the final say. KESCA certification confirms the interval was followed.

Our story

35 years making sure "cleaned" actually means cleaned.

KESCA was founded by Rob Sweet — in the indoor air quality trade since 1988, and tired of watching the industry sell stickers instead of doing the work.

  1. 1988

    Into the trade

    Rob enters Canada's indoor air quality industry — exhaust systems, ductwork, and the parts of buildings most people never see.

  2. 1991

    Bill C-281

    MP John Manley tables the Indoor Air Quality Act in Parliament. Rob takes its language as his job description and starts building courses to prevent loss of life and damage to property.

  3. 2004

    KESCA & Air Advocates registered

    Both registered as non-profits with one mission: verify the work, license the people doing it right, and give kitchens a registry they can trust.

  4. Today

    The standard

    A growing network of licensed contractors and certified locations — built on NFPA 96 and verified, twice-yearly inspections.

Kitchen exhaust cleaners were selling stickers instead of cleaning the systems. Same story with air duct cleaning, asbestos removal, mold remediation. HVAC companies weren't even cleaning systems because they wouldn't break down often enough. People paid big money — and no one was looking to see if the jobs were actually done.

— Rob Sweet, founder & certified instructor since 1991

Questions, answered

Frequently asked

What is a kitchen exhaust system?
The hood, ductwork and fan above a commercial stove that pulls grease, smoke, heat, fumes and combustion products out of the kitchen. Grease collects inside it over time — which is exactly why it has to be cleaned on a schedule.
How often does it need to be cleaned?
The NFPA 96 standard recommends kitchen exhaust filters be cleaned weekly, and the full system inspected by a licensed member twice a year. This prevents grease fires and keeps the air, water, equipment and environment clean.
Why should a cleaning contractor become licensed?
Only a licensed contractor’s work is recognized by fire, health, building and insurance inspectors. Licensing grows your customer base — and protects you: if work isn’t performed to the guidelines, it’s your liability insurance that covers the cost, and you risk violations, suspension or fines.
Does a restaurant have to hire a KESCA member to clean?
No. Owners and staff can take the free course and clean to standard themselves. But every food-service location with an exhaust system does need a licensed KESCA member to inspect it twice a year.
